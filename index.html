Productivity Techniques and Principles

Learn about different strategies for being productive and efficient in your work!

Eisenhower Method

The Eisenhower Method helps you prioritize tasks by their urgency and importance.

Tasks that are both urgent and important should be completed first.

Important but not urgent should be scheduled to complete at a later time.

Urgent but not important should be prioritized last or delegated.

Neither urgent or important should be deleted from your to-do list.

Pomodoro Technique

The Pomodoro technique incorporates breaks into your work flow to help you have better focus.

To implement the technique, you need to first establish the task you want to work on.

Set a timer for 25 minutes and work on your task with total focus during this period.

Take a 5 minute break to do whatever you want, and you've completed one round or "Pomodoro."

Repeat these steps and give yourself a longer 15-25 minute break after completing four Pomodoros.

Pareto Analysis

With Pareto analysis, you can identify your most helpful and harmful routines and work more efficiently.

Analyze your routines with the 80/20 principle: 20 percent of your routines lead to 80 percent of your outcomes.

List routines that you implement for a certain desired outcome. Identify the 20% that lead to 80% of the outcome.

List routines that contribute to a certain undesired outcome. Identify the 20% that lead to 80% of the outcome.

Focus your time and energy on the first set of routines you identified, and eliminate the other set.

Parkinson's Law

Parkinson's Law is based on the idea that work will expand to fill the time allotted for it.

If you give yourself two weeks to finish a project, you are likely going to use the entire two weeks.

If you give yourself one week instead, you are then likely to finish the project in that time frame.

Always give yourself less time than you think you need to complete tasks.

The work you produce won't be perfect, but you will have more time afterwards to refine it.

2-Minute Rule

The 2-Minute Rule breaks tasks down into small components, making it easier to get started.

If a task will take two minutes or less to complete, do it immediately.

If a task will take longer than two minutes, spend two minutes working on it.

If you get distracted, stop and take two minutes to think before continuing to engage with the distraction.

Big projects are daunting, but getting started for two minutes builds momentum you can use to finish.
